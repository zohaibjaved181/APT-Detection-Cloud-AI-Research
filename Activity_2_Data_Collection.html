📁 Activity 2: Data Collection Methods

Selecting Two Data Collection Methods

With a quantitative methodology in place, we now need to decide how we will actually gather the data our AI models require. For a study like ours, the data collection strategy must provide large-scale, structured, and labelled datasets that support rigorous training and testing.

💾

Method 1: Secondary Data Analysis

Publicly Available Benchmark Datasets

We draw on established, peer-reviewed network intrusion datasets that the cybersecurity research community has validated over many years. These contain both normal traffic and carefully labelled attack samples, giving us a solid foundation for model training and direct comparison with published work.

CICIDS 2017/2018 UNSW-NB15 Unraveled APT DARPA Transparent Computing
🧪

Method 2: Controlled Experiment

Simulated Cloud Environment with Live Attacks

We build a cloud-based testbed environment and run realistic attack campaigns against it using established adversary simulation frameworks. This produces fresh, cloud-native data that captures modern threat behaviours not found in older benchmark datasets.

AWS/Azure Testbed MITRE ATT&CK Mapping Atomic Red Team Custom APT Scenarios

Why two methods instead of one? Existing benchmark datasets give us established baselines so we can compare fairly with prior work. Our simulated environment fills a well-documented gap in the literature by producing cloud-specific APT data that older datasets simply do not contain.

🔍 Detailed Justification

Why These Methods Work for Our Study

Each method addresses a different aspect of our data needs. Together they ensure we have both the breadth for fair comparison and the depth for cloud-specific validation.

💾 Secondary Data — The Case For It

  • ✓ Community-validated — These datasets have been used in hundreds of published studies, which means our results can be directly compared to existing work
  • ✓ Pre-labelled attacks — Each record is already tagged with its traffic type, eliminating the need for manual annotation and reducing human bias
  • ✓ Massive scale — Millions of network flow records provide enough data volume for deep learning models that are notoriously data-hungry
  • ✓ Reproducibility — Anyone can download the same data and replicate our experiments, which is essential for credible research

How it helps our research: These datasets serve as the training foundation and allow us to benchmark our proposed model against state-of-the-art methods using identical evaluation conditions.

🧪 Controlled Experiment — The Case For It

  • ✓ Cloud-native patterns — Captures API abuse, container escape attempts, and lateral movement tactics that are unique to cloud infrastructure
  • ✓ Current threat landscape — Simulates 2024-2025 APT tactics, techniques, and procedures that older benchmark datasets simply cannot reflect
  • ✓ Full variable control — We decide exactly which attacks to execute, at what times, and at what intensity, giving us precise ground truth labels
  • ✓ Addresses a research gap — Multiple review papers have noted the lack of cloud-specific APT datasets as a major limitation in the field

How it helps our research: The simulated environment validates that our model works not just on historical data, but also on fresh, realistic cloud scenarios that represent the threats organisations actually face today.

Why This Combination Works

  • ✓ Breadth meets depth — Benchmarks give us breadth for fair comparison; the experiment gives us depth for cloud-specific validation
  • ✓ Historical meets current — We test against both established attack patterns and the latest APT techniques in cloud environments
  • ✓ Community meets novelty — Published datasets let peers replicate our work; simulation data contributes something genuinely new to the field
  • ✓ Aligns with methodology — Both methods produce structured, numerical data perfectly suited to quantitative analysis

Final thought: By combining a well-established data source with a purpose-built experimental environment, we cover both the rigour expected by the academic community and the practical relevance demanded by industry. Neither method alone would be sufficient, but together they provide exactly the evidence base our research questions require.
