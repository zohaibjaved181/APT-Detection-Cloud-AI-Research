📋 Activity 1: Research Methodology Selection

Choosing the Right Research Methodology

For our study on detecting Advanced Persistent Threats in cloud networks through artificial intelligence, we need a methodology that allows us to measure, test, and validate system performance in a structured way.

Our Research Topic

We are building an AI-based detection system that identifies sophisticated, multi-stage cyberattacks in cloud environments. The work involves training machine learning models on network traffic data, testing them against known attack scenarios, and measuring how well they perform compared to existing tools.

This is fundamentally an engineering and experimental research problem that requires numerical evidence and controlled testing.

What We Need From a Methodology

  • ✓ Ability to run controlled experiments with measurable outcomes
  • ✓ Statistical tools to compare different AI models objectively
  • ✓ Clear, reproducible results that other researchers can verify
  • ✓ Structured approach to testing hypotheses about detection performance

⚖ Comparing Approaches

Qualitative vs. Quantitative vs. Mixed Methods

Before settling on one approach, it helps to understand what each methodology offers and where it falls short for our specific research problem.

📝

Qualitative

Focuses on understanding experiences, meanings, and context through interviews, observations, and thematic analysis.

WHEN IT WORKS

Exploring how security analysts perceive threats or make decisions under pressure.

WHY NOT FOR US

We need hard numbers on detection rates, not subjective interpretations. Our AI models produce measurable outputs.

📊

Quantitative

SELECTED

Relies on numerical data, statistical analysis, and measurable variables. Tests hypotheses through structured experiments.

WHY IT FITS

Our entire research revolves around measuring model accuracy, comparing algorithms statistically, and producing reproducible benchmarks.

LIMITATION

Does not capture human factors in threat response. We accept this trade-off since our focus is the AI system itself.

🔀

Mixed Methods

Combines quantitative experiments with qualitative insights. Uses both numerical data and participant perspectives.

WHEN IT WORKS

When you also want to explore usability of the detection dashboard or gather analyst feedback on alerts.

WHY NOT FOR US

Adds complexity without clear benefit at this stage. Our primary question is about model performance, not user experience.

✅ Justification

Why Quantitative Methodology is the Best Fit

The nature of our research problem naturally demands a quantitative approach. Here is a clear breakdown of why this choice makes sense.

The Core Argument

At its heart, our research asks: "Can an AI model reliably detect advanced persistent threats in cloud network traffic?" Answering this question requires us to:

  • 1 Train models on large-scale datasets containing thousands of network flow records
  • 2 Measure detection performance using established metrics like precision, recall, and F1-score
  • 3 Compare our results against existing methods using the same benchmark conditions
  • 4 Apply statistical tests to confirm that observed improvements are not due to chance

Alignment with Research Goals

Research Goal How Quantitative Supports It
Build detection framework Provides metrics to evaluate whether the system actually works
Beat existing methods Statistical comparison gives objective evidence of improvement
Ensure real-time viability Inference latency measured in milliseconds, not opinions
Validate across scenarios Cross-validation and multiple datasets ensure generalizability

In plain terms: We chose quantitative methodology because our research produces numbers (accuracy scores, processing times, error rates), and we need statistical tools to make sense of those numbers and draw valid conclusions.

📁 Activity 2: Data Collection Methods

How We Collect Our Data

For a quantitative study like ours, the data collection strategy needs to provide large-scale, structured, and reliable datasets. We have selected two complementary methods that together give us comprehensive coverage.

💾

Method 1: Secondary Data

(Benchmark Datasets)

We use publicly available, peer-reviewed network traffic datasets that contain both normal activity and labelled APT attack samples. These are widely accepted in the cybersecurity research community.

CICIDS 2017/2018 UNSW-NB15 Unraveled APT DARPA TC
🧪

Method 2: Controlled Experiment

(Simulated Cloud Environment)

We set up a cloud testbed and simulate realistic APT attack campaigns using the MITRE ATT&CK framework. This generates fresh, cloud-native data that reflects modern threat behaviors.

AWS/Azure Testbed MITRE ATT&CK Atomic Red Team Custom Scenarios

Why two methods? Existing benchmark datasets give us established baselines for comparison, while our simulated environment produces data that specifically reflects cloud-native APT tactics that older datasets simply do not cover.

🔍 Deep Dive

Justifying Each Collection Method

Each method addresses a different gap in our data needs. Together, they provide a complete picture for training, testing, and validating our AI models.

💾 Secondary Data (Benchmark Datasets)

  • ✓ Widely validated — used by hundreds of studies, enabling direct comparison with published results
  • ✓ Pre-labelled — attack types are already annotated, reducing manual effort and human bias
  • ✓ Large scale — millions of network flows providing enough samples for deep learning models
  • ✓ Reproducible — other researchers can replicate our experiments using the same data

How it helps: These datasets serve as our training foundation and allow us to benchmark our model against state-of-the-art methods using the same evaluation conditions.

🧪 Controlled Experiment (Cloud Simulation)

  • ✓ Cloud-native — captures API abuse, container escape, and lateral movement patterns unique to cloud
  • ✓ Current threat landscape — simulates 2024-2025 APT tactics that older datasets miss entirely
  • ✓ Controlled variables — we decide exactly which attacks to run, when, and at what intensity
  • ✓ Fills a research gap — addresses the documented lack of cloud-specific APT datasets in literature

How it helps: The simulated environment validates that our model works not just on historical data, but also on fresh, realistic cloud attack scenarios that represent current threats.
